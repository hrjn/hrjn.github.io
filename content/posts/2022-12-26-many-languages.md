Almost every programmer has dabbled with more than one programming language in their career. There are various reasons for it: pure curiosity, work requirements, expanding your skills to steer you career, you name it. However, once you start getting comfortable with your "main" language, it can be harder to put yourself in the beginner's shoes again and learn a new one. "It's going to be hard", "I want to focus on mastering my main language first", these are the excuses I could typically come up with, but then I remember that I have actually been doing some similar concurrent learning multiples times in the past.

In college, I started my programming journey with C, a simple low-level and battle-tested language. Since I majored in Electrical Engineering, I also had to code some [VHDL](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/VHDL) to describe logic circuits, I even had the painful pleasure to write [68k assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motorola_68000) for a microprocessors course. At a higher level, I also took a Java course to learn the basics of object-oriented programming, and I implemented my share of Matlab scripts for various signal processing assignments. In grad school, I discovered the world of high-performance computing by implementing sampling algorithms in Fortran90 which pleasantly surprised me with its intuitive syntax. That's a lot of different languages over a few years, and yet it didn't drive me crazy :)

Even before that, from middle-school to high-school I had French, English and Spanish classes (I could have gone even further and add Latin and ancient Greek to the list but I was too lazy). A few years later in college, I started learning Japanese through elective courses. Granted that it's less about programming, but it's still about languages and how you can learn to express the same ideas in different words while learning about the similarities and subtle differences between idioms. Learning several programming languages somehow targets the same goal: getting better in *programming* by walking different paths for the same journeys.

In my case, I started looking into [Rust](https://www.rust-lang.org) because I wanted to go beyond my current main language (Python) and better understand low-level topics like memory management and systems programming. I feel like I just scratched the surface of these aspects during my college years, and now that I am ~~older~~ more experienced I hope to take a fresh look at those topics. It's been a fairly challenging-yet-awarding adventure so far, and I hope to write more about it soon !
